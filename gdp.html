    GDP per Capita

    GDP per capita is a widely used measure of average living standards and economic output per person. In this project, it acts as the core outcome variable because it captures the cross-country income gaps we want to explain. The dataset allows comparisons across countries and over time, making it possible to observe persistent inequality, regional clustering, and long-run development trajectories.

    Graph 1 Top 10 Countries by Total GDP (2022)

    This bar chart highlights how concentrated global economic output is among a small number of countries. First, the United States and China dominate the ranking, creating a large gap between the top two and the rest, which shows that the world economy is heavily shaped by a few very large producers. Second, several countries appear with similar magnitudes in the middle of the top ten, indicating that after the top tier there is a tighter group of large economies that still matter a lot but do not match the scale of the leaders. Third, the list includes countries with very different population sizes, which is a reminder that total GDP reflects economic scale, not living standards, and motivates why GDP per capita is needed to evaluate prosperity per person.

    Graph 2 Top 10 Countries by GDP per Capita (2022)

    This chart focuses on average income levels, which can look very different from the total GDP ranking. First, many of the highest GDP per capita locations are small economies, including financial centers and small states, which suggests that very high average incomes are not limited to the largest producers in the world economy. Second, the gap between the top entry and the rest is large, indicating that the top of the income distribution is extremely concentrated and that the richest cases can be outliers relative to other high-income countries. Third, this ranking helps interpret later scatter plots, because countries with extreme GDP per capita values can influence relationships and trend lines, so it is important to recognize where the high-end leverage may come from.

    Graph 3 GDP per Capita Distribution Across Countries (2022)

    This histogram shows the full cross-country distribution rather than only the top performers. First, most countries cluster at relatively low GDP per capita values, which indicates that low and middle income outcomes are the global norm rather than the exception. Second, the distribution has a long right tail extending toward very high incomes, meaning that a small number of countries are far above the rest, which makes global inequality highly asymmetric. Third, the shape implies that comparing simple averages can be misleading, because the upper tail can pull summary statistics upward, so later analyses should pay attention to dispersion, outliers, and the possibility that relationships differ between the dense lower-income mass and the high-income tail.
