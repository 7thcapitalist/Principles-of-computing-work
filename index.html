    Why Are Some Countries Richer Than Others?

    Economic inequality between countries is one of the most persistent features of the global economy. Some nations consistently generate high incomes and sustained growth, while others remain stuck at low levels of development. This project tests whether innovation helps explain these differences and whether innovation can be influenced through policy choices. Using cross-country data on GDP per capita, the Global Innovation Index (GII), and R&D expenditure, we build a chain from outcomes to mechanisms, then from mechanisms to controllable inputs.

    Graph 1 Global GDP per Capita Map

    This map highlights three central features of global income differences. First, GDP per capita varies enormously across countries, with some economies producing several times more income per person than others. Second, these differences are geographically clustered, as high income countries tend to concentrate in specific regions while low income countries dominate others. Third, the persistence of these clusters across time suggests that income differences are not short term fluctuations, but reflect deeper structural patterns in the global economy.

    Graph 2 GDP per Capita Distribution

    The distribution of GDP per capita reveals three important insights about inequality. First, most countries are concentrated at relatively low income levels, indicating that high income status is not the global norm. Second, a small number of countries occupy the upper tail of the distribution, capturing a disproportionate share of global prosperity. Third, the strong right skew of the distribution shows that income differences are large and uneven, reinforcing the idea that global inequality is structural rather than marginal.

    Graph 3 Innovation Index Distribution

    The Innovation Index distribution shows that innovative capacity is not evenly spread across countries. First, innovation performance varies widely, with a small group of countries achieving very high scores. Second, many countries cluster at lower innovation levels, indicating limited innovation output across much of the world. Third, the overall shape of the distribution closely resembles that of income, suggesting a possible connection between innovation outcomes and economic development.

    Graph 4 Innovation Index vs GDP per Capita

    This scatter plot provides three key observations about innovation and income. First, there is a strong positive relationship between innovation scores and GDP per capita, indicating that more innovative countries tend to be richer. Second, high income countries are tightly clustered at high innovation levels, while low income countries are concentrated at lower scores. Third, the limited number of rich but low innovation countries suggests that sustained prosperity is rarely achieved without strong innovation performance.

    Graph 5 Innovation Index vs GDP Growth

    This visualization reveals how innovation relates to economic dynamics over time. First, countries with higher innovation scores tend to experience stronger or more stable GDP growth rates. Second, low innovation countries often show weaker or more volatile growth performance. Third, the pattern suggests that innovation is associated not only with income levels, but also with the ability to sustain economic expansion over longer periods.

    Graph 6 R&D Expenditure Distribution

    The distribution of R&D expenditure highlights major differences in innovation investment. First, countries allocate very different shares of GDP to research and development, ranging from near zero to substantial levels. Second, high R&D investment is relatively rare and concentrated among a small group of economies. Third, this wide variation indicates that innovation inputs reflect deliberate choices rather than uniform global standards.

    Graph 7 R&D Expenditure Map

    This map shows how R&D investment is distributed across countries and regions. First, high R&D spending tends to cluster in the same regions that exhibit high income levels. Second, many low income regions also display consistently low R&D investment. Third, the geographic overlap between R&D and income suggests a close connection between innovation inputs and broader economic outcomes.

    Graph 8 R&D Expenditure vs Innovation Index

    This scatter plot provides direct evidence on the link between investment and innovation. First, higher R&D expenditure is strongly associated with higher innovation scores across countries. Second, there is noticeable dispersion around the trend, indicating that countries with similar investment can achieve different outcomes. Third, this variation implies that while R&D is important, other factors influence how effectively investment is converted into innovation.

    Graph 9 Innovation Efficiency

    The efficiency metric highlights differences in how countries transform R&D into innovation. First, some countries generate high innovation outcomes relative to their level of investment. Second, others invest heavily but achieve comparatively weaker innovation performance. Third, this spread suggests that institutional quality, human capital, and economic structure play a role in shaping innovation efficiency beyond spending alone.

    Graph 10 R&D Expenditure vs GDP per Capita

    This visualization links innovation inputs directly to income outcomes. First, countries that invest more in R&D generally exhibit higher GDP per capita. Second, low R&D investment is rarely associated with high income levels. Third, the consistent positive relationship reinforces the idea that innovation investment aligns closely with economic prosperity across countries.

    Graph 11 Bubble Chart

    This chart combines R&D investment, innovation outcomes, and income into a single view. First, countries with high R&D and high innovation tend to have the largest GDP per capita values. Second, countries with low values on both dimensions are typically among the poorest. Third, the presence of intermediate cases highlights variation in development paths and reinforces differences in efficiency and economic structure.

    Graph 12 Regional Comparison

    Regional boxplots reveal systematic differences in innovation and investment across the world. First, some regions consistently display higher median innovation and R&D levels than others. Second, regions with lower medians also show tighter distributions, suggesting limited access to innovation capacity. Third, these regional gaps help explain why global inequality persists, as countries operate within broader structural environments.
