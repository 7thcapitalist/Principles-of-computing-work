    Why Are Some Countries Richer Than Others?

    This project starts from a simple but fundamental question in economics: why do some countries enjoy much higher incomes than others? To explore this, I build a cross-country dataset that tracks differences in innovation capacity and income from 2011 to 2024. The idea is to move from raw data to meaningful insights about global inequality. By combining information on innovation performance and GDP per capita, the project investigates whether richer countries are systematically those that innovate more, and whether innovation might be one of the mechanisms behind persistent income gaps across nations.

    From Innovation Structures to Innovation Output

    The first step in the analysis focuses on how governments try to create the conditions for innovation. Using policy-based data on R&D support, the project looks at how much countries invest in innovation structures such as research incentives and public support for R&D. However, spending alone does not guarantee results. To measure how effectively countries convert these structures into real innovation, I rely on the Global Innovation Index (GII), which scores national innovation ecosystems on a 0–100 scale. By comparing R&D support to GII scores, the project examines which countries have efficient innovation systems and which ones struggle to turn policies and resources into actual innovative output.

    Does Innovation Help Explain Global Inequality?

    In the final stage, the project links innovation performance to economic well-being using World Bank data on GDP per capita. After merging the Global Innovation Index with GDP per capita by country–year, I estimate a linear regression model where the dependent variable is the logarithm of GDP per capita and the key explanatory variable is the GII score. This allows me to quantify how strongly higher innovation capacity is associated with higher income levels. The goal is not to prove causality, but to test whether countries that innovate more also tend to be richer, and whether innovation is a plausible contributor to the income gaps we see across the world.
